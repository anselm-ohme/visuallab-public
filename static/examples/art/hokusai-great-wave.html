Katsushika Hokusai

About this work

The first print in Hokusai's series Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji, this woodblock print is the most widely reproduced image in the history of art. Mount Fuji appears small and snow-capped on the horizon, its silhouette echoed by the cresting wave that dominates the foreground.

The print uses a then-newly imported Prussian blue pigment, which gave Hokusai a deep, lightfast colour unavailable to earlier ukiyo-e artists. The Met's impression is one of the finest surviving early printings.

Several thousand impressions were originally pulled from the woodblocks; only a few hundred remain. The image's reach is so total that it has become a visual shorthand for Japan itself, despite originally being a piece of mass-produced commercial art.

About the artist

Katsushika Hokusai (1760-1849) was a Japanese ukiyo-e painter and printmaker of the Edo period. Active for over seventy years, he produced an estimated 30,000 works. He famously claimed that 'from around the age of 73 I began to grasp the structures of birds and beasts'.
